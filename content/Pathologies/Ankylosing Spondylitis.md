# Ankylosing Spondylitis
## Background
### Definitions
Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) is a chronic, inflammatory disease affecting the axial spine. It is a disease within the group of disorders known as spondyloarthritis which also includes:
- Nonradiographic axial spondyloarthritis
- Undifferentiated spondyloarthritis
- Reactive arthritis
- Arthritis associated with IBD/[[Psoriatic Arthritis|psoriasis]].

AS is characterized by chronic back pain and progressive stiffness. AS also has a range of extra-spinal involvement and presents typically in younger individuals (<40).
### Etiology and Pathogenesis
The cause of ankylosing spondylitis is largely unknown. There is genetic factors that increase an individual's susceptibility to ankylosing spondylitis.

The pathogenesis of AS is thought to involve:
- GI mucosal lesions and gut microbial invasion to submucosa and systemic circulation
- Generation of IL17A and other inflammatory cytokines
- IL-17A-producing cells travel from guts to the entheses and joints

Microinjuries from mechanical stress causes activation of resident immune cells which causes inflammatory reaction, even in individuals without arthritis.

Inflammation results in bone erosions, particularly in the vertebral body, leading to osteoporosis. Presence of inflammatory markers stimulate pain receptors resulting in characteristic symptoms.

However, unlike rheumatoid arthritis, where only bone erosion is observed, new bone formation occurs at the entheses. Bone formation increase spinal rigidity and, in later stages, fusion of the spine.

While disease process primarily occurs in the axial joints (vertebral column and SI joints), peripheral joints may be affected asymmetrically. Systemic activation of immune system can also affect extra-articular organs.
## Clinical Presentation
### Signs & Symptoms
**Spinal and peripheral joint involvement**
AS is characterized by involvement of:
- SI joints and vertebra
- Hip and shoulder
- Peripheral joints
and:
- Costcovertebral, manubriosternal, sternoclavicular, costochondral inflammation
- Extraspinal enthesis
- Dactylitis

Pain is the most common complaint:
- Insidious onset
- Improves with exercise/movement
- No improvement with rest
- Pain at night
and is typically reported as (in order of prevalance):
- Back/neck pain
- Buttock pain
- Hip pain
- Peripheral joint pain
- Enthesitis

Other findings of advanced disease include:
- Impaired spinal mobility and chest expansion
- Postural abnormalities (hyperhyphosis)

**Extra-articular involvement**
Neurological:
- Compression of sacral nerve roots
- Bladder and bowel incontinence
Ocular (anterior uveitis):
- Decreased visual acuity
- Eye pain
- Lacrimation
- Photophobia
Cardiac:
- Aortitis
- Aortic regurgitation
- Conduction abnormalities
Respiratory (rare):
- Apical lung fibrosis (from interstitial tissue inflammation)
GI:
- Asymptomatic ulceration of ileum and colon
- Increased risk of IBD
Renal:
- Secondary amyloidosis due to reposition of amyloid proteins (acute phase reactant from inflammation)
### History & Physical Exam
History should include character of pain, confirming its inflammatory nature.

A comprehensive evaluation is recommended owing to the systemic nature of the disease.
### Risk Factors
In addition to genetic predisposition, a number of comorbidities are associated with ankylosing spondylitis:
- Psychosocial status
- Fibromyalgia
- Osteopenia
- Cardiovascular disease
- Pulmonary disease
## Diagnosis
### Criteria
Diagnosis is made clinically based on the pattern of clinical findings with support of laboratory and imaging findings.
### Work-up
**Initial testing for disease and complications/comorbidities**
- CRP
- CBC
- Renal function tests
- Liver chemistries

**Imaging**
- Plain radiography (findings: bamboo spine in late disease)
### Differential
Other diagnoses include:
- Other causes of [[Low Back Pain|back pain]]
- Fibromyalgia and myalgia
- Diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis (DISH)
- Vertebral compression fracture
- SI joint infection
### Red Flags / Complications
Complications include spinal and extra-articular manifestations.

Fractures:
- Vertebral fracture due to fragility; most commonly lower cervical spine and during extension
- Non-vertebral fracture (less clear link)

Neurologic manifestations:
- Spinal cord injury
- Atlantoaxial subluxation
- Cauda equina syndrome

Other complications include:
- Kidney disease
- Adverse pregnancy outcomes (eg. prematurity)
## Management
### Initial Treatment
**Nonpharmacological interventions**
All newly diagnosed patients should receive:
- Patient education regarding disease, exercise, posture-training, management of comorbidities
- Smoking cessation
- Depression screening and psychosocial support
- Exercise and physical therapy

**Pharmacological treatment** consist of symptom management with NSAIDs. Options of equal effectiveness are:
- Naproxen (up to 500 mg BID)
- Celecoxib (up to 200 mg BID)
- Ibuprofen (up to 800 mg TID)

NSAIDs should be used as needed but treatment asymptomatically is not recommended due to other risks. There is inconclusive evidence that prophylactic NSAID use may prevent growth of syndesmophytes in the spine and as such not the standard of care.
### Inadequate Response to Initial Treatment
In patients who do not gain adequate relief from NSAIDs (with two different NSAIDs trialed), DMARDs can be trialed.
- TNF inhibitors
- IL-17 inhibitors
- JAK inhibitors (2nd line after above two)
